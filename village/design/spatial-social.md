# Design Law 3 — Spatial Social

**Movement is the cost of contact.**

Inherits from [`underspecified-surface.md`](./underspecified-surface.md).

---

## The law

To reach another child, the child must move through the village to where the other child is. Contact is spatial, not indexical. There is no tap-a-name-and-connect. There is: *"Let's go check at the bridge."*

Movement is the cost of contact. The cost is deliberate. It restores to child friendship the shape it had for the whole of human history until roughly 2007: **you went to find your friend.**

---

## Why

Instant contact is the current-state harm. The child who can reach any other child at any time, with no cost, learns that people are always available and never require effort to reach. The friendship muscle — the practice of walking to the friend's house, of showing up, of finding them at the field, of missing them if they were not there — atrophies. The child grows into an adult who cannot understand why relationships require effort, because their formative years taught them that people are cheap and always in-hand.

Spatial social restores the cost. Not as friction for friction's sake. As the shape of friendship the child is being formed for.

---

## What the village looks like

A place. Simple. Blank at v1, filled in by the children and by open-source contributors over time.

- **A square.** The default gathering place. Children arrive here when they enter common-time.
- **A bridge.** A crossing. A place children agree to meet.
- **A field.** Open space for movement.
- **Forts.** Each child can build one. Their own space. Small, simple, theirs.
- **Ziplines and paths.** The connective tissue between places. Movement is not instant.

At v1 the geometry is minimal. Text-and-voice, with the child's imagination doing the work (Law 0). Later, open-source contributors will build richer worlds inside this substrate — different villages, different terrains, different themes. The doctrine constrains what any village must preserve: **places, movement between places, presence at places, imagination filling the rest.**

---

## What movement looks like

The child says: *"Take me to the bridge."* The village moves them. They do not walk with a joystick; the village is not a walking simulator. But movement takes **time.** Reaching the bridge is not instant. The child hears the passage — a soft cue that they are in transit — and arrives when arrival happens. Ten seconds. Twenty. Long enough that reaching a friend is a small act, short enough that a child's patience holds.

The transit time is not a loading screen. It is the point. It is the space in which the child forms the intention to see the friend, holds the friend in mind, and imagines what they will say.

---

## Presence, not availability

A child in the village is either **at a place** or **in transit.** They are never "online" in the ambient always-available sense. Other children can only find them at a place. If a child is at their fort, friends who want to see them go to the fort. If the child leaves for the bridge, they are no longer at the fort and cannot be reached there.

This mirrors the world. If you go to your friend's house and they are not home, they are not home. You do not text them from the porch. You come back later, or you go somewhere else, or you find them at the field. The child is being formed for that shape of presence.

---

## No global directory

- No list of all children in the village.
- No "who is online now" surface.
- No search by name across the whole bubble.
- To reach a child you do not know where to find, you ask another child: *"Have you seen Marc?"* The answer comes in voice, from a child who knows.

The social graph is discovered by asking, not by browsing.

---

## What this refuses

- It refuses instant connection between arbitrary children.
- It refuses "presence" indicators that show ambient availability.
- It refuses friend lists as browsable surfaces.
- It refuses always-on group chats. Groups form at places, and dissolve when the children leave the place.

---

## What this commits to

- Every contact between children costs movement.
- Every friendship requires the small act of going to find the friend.
- The village is a place first, a network second.
- Imagination fills in what the rendering leaves undone (Law 0).

---

## The generational stake

Children formed on spatial social will grow up understanding that friendship is a **practice** — something you do, somewhere, with your body and your attention, not something that streams into your pocket. That understanding is the foundation of every functional community humans have ever built, and it is the piece that the current substrate has been quietly dissolving.

The village restores it, on the device that would otherwise complete the dissolution.

**Pour le bien-être du peuple.**
