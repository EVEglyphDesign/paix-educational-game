# Design Law 0 — The Underspecified Surface

**The axiom the other six design laws inherit from.**

---

## The law

The village renders less than a child expects. This is intentional.

Every rendering the platform provides is a rendering the child does not have to perform. Every rendering the platform refuses is a rendering the child must construct.

The construction is the training. The training is the point.

**High-resolution surfaces produce low-resolution imaginations. Low-resolution surfaces produce high-resolution imaginations.**

The village is deliberately low-resolution.

---

## Why this axiom sits above the others

The tech generation that built the current substrate — the founders, architects, and systems thinkers who came out of the 8-bit and 16-bit era — was formed by screens that were structurally incapable of rendering the world. Sixteen colors. Chunky sprites. Three pixels for a bird. The screen refused to render; the child was required to render. That requirement built the interior cognitive muscle those adults now run their companies on.

The current generation of children's screens does the opposite. Photoreal rendering at 120fps, dopamine timing tuned to the millisecond, nothing left for the child to construct. The interior world atrophies because the exterior world never stops feeding it.

This is the actual harm underneath the "screen time" panic. Not the hours. The **resolution.** High-resolution rendering displaces interior rendering. The muscle does not develop. The generation that is being fed high-resolution surfaces will not produce the founders, architects, and systems thinkers the substrate integration will require.

The village refuses this. On purpose.

---

## How the axiom shows up in the other laws

- **Law 1 (Time partition)** — lesson time is protected from social interruption so the child's interior world can form without fragmentation.
- **Law 2 (Voice-first)** — the screen refuses to render the friend. The child constructs the friend in imagination from voice alone.
- **Law 3 (Spatial social)** — the village is a place with simple geometry. Bridges, forts, ziplines exist as concepts the child holds, not as rendered assets that hold the child.
- **Law 4 (Service and evidence)** — the child gathers evidence from the real, unrendered world. Nothing is pre-composed.
- **Law 5 (Earned broadcast)** — the network refuses to render the child's voice to the group by default. The child imagines the group first, and earns the right to reach it.
- **Law 6 (Status-signal alignment)** — the village refuses to render status through visible metrics. The child imagines who is admirable by watching what actually happens.

Every law in this lane can be read as: *what does the village refuse to render, and what does that refusal ask the child to build inside themselves?*

---

## What the village will and will not render

**Will render:**

- Simple orientation cues. "Your friend is near the bridge." Text only. No mini-map with icons.
- Voice, faithfully. The child's own voice and their friends' voices come through cleanly, because voice is the surface.
- The child's evidence uploads (photos of the raked lawn, the shoveled walk) — because evidence is real and must be seen.
- The child's own creations — their fort, their glyph, their mark on the village.

**Will not render:**

- Photoreal avatars. The child's presence in the village is a callsign glyph and a voice. Not a face, not a body, not clothing. The other children imagine the child from the glyph and the voice, the way friends have always imagined each other over any distance.
- Animated feed content, autoplay video, notification badges, red dots.
- Any surface designed to be looked at for its own sake.
- Any rendering whose purpose is to hold the child's attention rather than to help the child do something specific and then look away.

The screen is a compass. A compass shows you where to go. It does not hold you.

---

## What this refuses

The village refuses the entire generation of design principles that have governed children's software for twenty years:

- It refuses "engagement" as a metric.
- It refuses "session length" as a metric.
- It refuses "retention" as a metric.
- It refuses high-fidelity rendering as a design goal.
- It refuses the belief that children's platforms compete on production values.

The village competes on **what it leaves undone.**

---

## What this commits to

- Every design decision inside the village must be tested against this law before being adopted.
- If a feature increases what the platform renders on behalf of the child, the feature must justify itself against the imagination cost.
- If a feature decreases what the platform renders and asks the child to fill in more, the feature is presumptively aligned with the axiom.
- The default direction of village evolution is **less, not more.**

---

## The generational stake

The children formed by this substrate will be the founders and architects of 2050. The reason is the same reason the Zelda generation became the founders and architects of 2015: the substrate they were formed on **required them to imagine, rather than doing the imagining for them.**

The village is a bet that we can restore that formation at scale, for a generation that would otherwise be formed by high-resolution surfaces designed to render everything and require nothing.

**Pour le bien-être du peuple.**
